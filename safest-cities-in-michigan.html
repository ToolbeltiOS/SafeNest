What the Michigan data shows

The typical Michigan city against the national baseline

The median ranked Michigan city reports 272.5 violent crimes per 100,000 residents — 28% below the national baseline of 381 that SafeNest grades against. Its median property rate is 1,426.7 per 100,000. 62.6% of the state's ranked cities report a violent rate below the national average.

How the grades fall

Across those 115 cities: 32 grade A, 30 B, 30 C, 15 D and 8 F. That puts 27.8% of Michigan's ranked cities at an A, against 38.5% of comparable cities nationally — below the national rate. The grade is national-relative, not a curve within the state: a city at exactly the US average violent and property crime rate scores 50 out of 100 and receives a C, wherever it is.

What the ranking leaves out

Three groups of Michigan places are deliberately absent, because including them would put the wrong names at the top:

  • 57 county and sheriff agencies. The FBI's data is keyed by agency, not by city, and county-wide agencies sit in it alongside municipal ones. Their averages are diluted by everything outside the urban core, so they outrank real cities without being cities.
  • 6 cities reporting implausibly little crime. An agency can file a full year and report almost nothing in it — one city in this database reports a single property crime for an entire year at a population of 16,584. That is a reporting gap, not safety, and it would otherwise take first place. Cities below the 5th percentile of the national property crime rate are excluded.
  • 3 cities whose most recent full year is not 2024. Ranking one city's older data against another's 2024 is not a comparison.

Why no small towns are on this list. Michigan has 296 municipalities under 10,000 residents in the FBI data. 57.4% of them are graded A, and 16.6% reported no violent crime at all. In a town of a few thousand people that is the expected outcome in a given year rather than evidence of anything, and a single incident would move the rate sharply. Ranking them against cities would put towns at the top of this page on the strength of a number that is mostly noise, so they are excluded from the ranking and reported here instead.

Methodology

Source. FBI Uniform Crime Reporting (UCR), Summary Reporting System — "Offenses Known and Clearances by Arrest" (Return A), via Jacob Kaplan's concatenated agency panel on openICPSR (DOI 10.3886/E100707V22, V22). Figures are crimes reported to the FBI by each city's police agency, for 2024.

The grade. A city's score is 100 / (1 + severity), weighing its violent rate against a national baseline of 381 per 100,000 (70%) and its property rate against 1,954 per 100,000 (30%). The same code computes the grade here, in the SafeNest app and in our data pipeline.

Reproducing this. Every number on this page is computed by data-pipeline/analysis/generate_state_rankings.py from the database that ships in the app. The ranking is not editorial: it is a sort.

What this ranking is not. A city grade is a city-wide average of reported crime. It does not describe a street, a block or an address, and crime is concentrated unevenly inside every city — including the ones at the top of this list. Adjacent positions in the table are not meaningfully different from one another: a single reported incident can move a city several places. Read the ranking as a rough sort, not a verdict on any particular place.

What a grade is, and isn't

SafeNest grades are algorithmic summaries of public records — informational opinions, not statements of fact and not guarantees about any place. They describe a city as a whole, using crime that was reported. They are not a substitute for looking at a specific address, and they must not be used to judge a person.
