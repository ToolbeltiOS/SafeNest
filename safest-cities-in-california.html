What the California data shows

The typical California city against the national baseline

The median ranked California city reports 348.8 violent crimes per 100,000 residents — 8% below the national baseline of 381 that SafeNest grades against. Its median property rate is 1,673.7 per 100,000. 54.5% of the state's ranked cities report a violent rate below the national average.

How the grades fall

Across those 358 cities: 52 grade A, 97 B, 140 C, 59 D and 10 F. That puts 14.5% of California's ranked cities at an A, against 38.5% of comparable cities nationally — below the national rate. The grade is national-relative, not a curve within the state: a city at exactly the US average violent and property crime rate scores 50 out of 100 and receives a C, wherever it is.

What the ranking leaves out

Three groups of California places are absent from the ranking, because each would otherwise take a top place without being a safe city (why):

  • 37 county and sheriff agencies — county-wide averages are diluted by everything outside the urban core, so they outrank real cities without being cities.
  • 1 city reporting implausibly little crime — an agency can file a full year and report almost nothing in it. That is a reporting gap, not safety, and it would otherwise take first place.
  • 5 cities whose most recent full year is not 2024 — ranking one city's older data against another's is not a comparison.

Which way California moved

Year-over-year direction, as reported: of the 358 ranked California cities with a comparable prior year, 165 reported a lower violent crime rate than the year before and 124 reported a higher one, with 69 roughly level. That is what the agencies filed for 2024 against their own previous year — it is a record of what changed, not a projection of what happens next. 146 of 358 ranked California cities reported no homicides at all.

How close the letters are in California

13 of California's 358 ranked cities would print a different letter grade if their violent crime count changed by a single incident. That is not a flaw peculiar to California: every threshold system has a boundary and some cities sit on it. It is the concrete reason to treat the gap between adjacent grades — and adjacent positions in the table above — as noise rather than a finding.

The largest California cities in the ranking, by population — not by crime. Big cities carry higher reported rates almost everywhere, which is why a safest-cities list and a biggest-cities list look nothing alike.

Why no small towns are on this list. California has 91 municipalities under 10,000 residents in the FBI data. 35.2% of them are graded A, and 2.2% reported no violent crime at all. In a town of a few thousand people that is the expected outcome in a given year rather than evidence of anything, and a single incident would move the rate sharply. Ranking them against cities would put towns at the top of this page on the strength of a number that is mostly noise, so they are excluded from the ranking and reported here instead.

Method, in short

Rankings come from FBI Uniform Crime Reporting data (2024), via the Kaplan agency panel (DOI 10.3886/E100707V22). A city's score is 100 / (1 + severity), weighing its violent rate against a national baseline of 381 per 100,000 (70%) and its property rate against 1,954 per 100,000 (30%) — the same code that grades cities in the SafeNest app. Every number here is computed by generate_state_rankings.py; the ranking is a sort, not an edit.

A grade is not a verdict. It is a city-wide average of reported crime — an informational opinion, not a statement of fact, and not a description of any street, block or address. It may not be used for tenant screening, hiring or lending (Terms). Full sources and data vintages: methodology.
