What the Texas data shows

The typical Texas city against the national baseline

The median ranked Texas city reports 228.6 violent crimes per 100,000 residents — 40% below the national baseline of 381 that SafeNest grades against. Its median property rate is 1,525.3 per 100,000. 75.1% of the state's ranked cities report a violent rate below the national average.

How the grades fall

Across those 245 cities: 72 grade A, 79 B, 72 C, 18 D and 4 F. That puts 29.4% of Texas's ranked cities at an A, against 38.5% of comparable cities nationally — below the national rate. The grade is national-relative, not a curve within the state: a city at exactly the US average violent and property crime rate scores 50 out of 100 and receives a C, wherever it is.

What the ranking leaves out

Two groups of Texas places are deliberately absent, because including them would put the wrong names at the top:

  • 91 county and sheriff agencies. The FBI's data is keyed by agency, not by city, and county-wide agencies sit in it alongside municipal ones. Their averages are diluted by everything outside the urban core, so they outrank real cities without being cities.
  • 1 city reporting implausibly little crime. An agency can file a full year and report almost nothing in it — one city in this database reports a single property crime for an entire year at a population of 16,584. That is a reporting gap, not safety, and it would otherwise take first place. Cities below the 5th percentile of the national property crime rate are excluded.

Why no small towns are on this list. Texas has 444 municipalities under 10,000 residents in the FBI data. 54.3% of them are graded A, and 15.1% reported no violent crime at all. In a town of a few thousand people that is the expected outcome in a given year rather than evidence of anything, and a single incident would move the rate sharply. Ranking them against cities would put towns at the top of this page on the strength of a number that is mostly noise, so they are excluded from the ranking and reported here instead.

Methodology

Source. FBI Uniform Crime Reporting (UCR), Summary Reporting System — "Offenses Known and Clearances by Arrest" (Return A), via Jacob Kaplan's concatenated agency panel on openICPSR (DOI 10.3886/E100707V22, V22). Figures are crimes reported to the FBI by each city's police agency, for 2024.

The grade. A city's score is 100 / (1 + severity), weighing its violent rate against a national baseline of 381 per 100,000 (70%) and its property rate against 1,954 per 100,000 (30%). The same code computes the grade here, in the SafeNest app and in our data pipeline.

Reproducing this. Every number on this page is computed by data-pipeline/analysis/generate_state_rankings.py from the database that ships in the app. The ranking is not editorial: it is a sort.

What this ranking is not. A city grade is a city-wide average of reported crime. It does not describe a street, a block or an address, and crime is concentrated unevenly inside every city — including the ones at the top of this list. Adjacent positions in the table are not meaningfully different from one another: a single reported incident can move a city several places. Read the ranking as a rough sort, not a verdict on any particular place.

What a grade is, and isn't

SafeNest grades are algorithmic summaries of public records — informational opinions, not statements of fact and not guarantees about any place. They describe a city as a whole, using crime that was reported. They are not a substitute for looking at a specific address, and they must not be used to judge a person.
