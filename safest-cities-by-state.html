44 states. Each ranking covers municipalities of 10,000+ residents with their own police department, on 2024 FBI data.

Why 7 states are missing. Alaska, Delaware, District of Columbia, Hawaii, Montana, Nevada and North Dakota have fewer than 10 cities that qualify — most of their territory is policed by county or state agencies rather than by municipal departments, and what remains is too small a set to rank. A ranking built on a handful of cities is not a ranking, so we do not publish one.

How to read these rankings

A ranking is a sort, not a verdict. Three things are worth knowing before you use one:

  • The grade is national-relative, not a curve within the state. A city at exactly the US average violent and property crime rate scores 50 out of 100 and receives a C, wherever it sits. A state's list can therefore be topped by a C, and that is information, not a glitch.
  • Adjacent positions are not meaningfully different. A single reported incident can move a city several places. Read the top of a list as a group, not as a photo finish.
  • A city grade is a city-wide average of reported crime. It does not describe a street, a block or an address, and crime is concentrated unevenly inside every city — including the ones at the top of these lists.

Counties, sheriff's agencies, towns under 10,000 people, and agencies that filed a year while reporting almost no crime in it are all excluded, because each of them would otherwise take first place without being a safe city. Every state page shows exactly how many it excluded and why. The full method is on the methodology page.
